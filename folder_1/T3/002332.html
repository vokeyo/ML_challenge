Drake thats typical of most pay to hunt operations in this state they are all geared around getting the biggest bang for the buck( no pun intended).By that i mean they want as much cash on the table as they can get for the product right down to figuring what the net worth of every animal or bird thats shot eguates to.One turkey eguals-x One duck eguals -y and so forth.Its not geared around a statagey of pricing for the experience and the value of the hunt and thats the problem.There is alot of time and effort put forth by reputable services to produce an enjoyable hunt for their guests and have the price egual the experience and also have the number of hunts figured to the extent that it does not over tax the land and the game on it and there is where alot of your cost structure goes to on those places. its used to keep good staff on hand and to have the appropriate resources to manage the place so that it has a lifespan of longer than five or six seasons wich seems to be the limit of most poorely run setups.By the seventh or eighth season places like blackriver are usually down the tubes so if you look at its history youll probably find that either its a new comer and poorly run from inexperience or its about reached its shelf life. Its a shame but thats really how it is some guy owns some land or they get together and lease a bunch of property with dollar signs in their eyes, they set up and shoot it down then just let it die a slow torturous death while people who have paid good money look on.Bostick is a prime example of that.The only recourse you have is to do your reasearch on the place before hand to get an indication you are going to get what you pay for before you cut the check.What you are paying for is service and a reasonable opportunity to see game.You cant have one with out the other on a pay establishment with out dissapointing people if you are looking for a long term buisness and most just aren't looking too far into the future.When a place starts offering club memberships with extreme prices as well as day hunts they are usually already down the drain and are trying to get what they can while the gettins good.If you think about it how can you have both on one property ?Day hunters and club members with out some riff occuring amonsgst the ranks. the same staff has to handle both entities with the same ammount of time and effort put forth to both. How is that possible with a limited staff?The answer is its not!Not unless you have unlimited resources available for staffing and wildlife management.You would also need a tremendous ammount of property at your disposal so neither group, day hunt or member would interfere with one another otherwise both are beating down the same ground day in and day out.I have a long history in that buisness. you don't know me so you will have to take my word on this, i have worked with and on many places both top of the line and bottom of the barrel and just about everything in between and it is a coin toss unless you do your research. you have to look at the setup, how many hunts they run a year, wether the owner -operator is hands on or not, how much property is involved and how many different species they are hunting there.how long have the guides been associated with the place and how many years has it been in operation.This would be the case wether you booked another hunt in S.C. or texas or argentina.I'm sorry you had a bad experience but you just have to chalk it up to the learning curve as unfortunate as that is for those who utilise such establisments.
