DEC Assisted by SCA in Ridding Norton Basin of "Wrack"

A team of employees from the Department of Environmental Conservation (DEC) and interns from the Student Conservation Association (SCA) recently descended on the Norton Basin Natural Resources Area to remove an estimated 400 yards of "wrack." Wrack is composed of dead phragmities (a reed) and garbage. Using pitchforks, shovels and wheelbarrows, the team pursued their task with determination despite inclement weather brewing nearby.

Norton Basin NRA is located in Queens. DEC acquired the property in 1996 and has worked since then to protect and restore this fragile marsh habitat. About one acre each of the shoreline and upland marsh have been restored.

alters the structure and function
of diverse marsh ecosystems

Phragmities-commonly called "phrag"-has become a hazard to the Norton Basin marsh community, threatening some native plant species. As dead phrag is washed ashore by the tide, it smothers fragile plants such as Spartina patens and inhibits growth of other upland marsh plants. Leigh Vogel, DEC Marine Resources, said, "Our hope is that wrack removal from the system will free up space for the reestablishment of high marsh vegetation such as salt grass, sea lavender and salt meadow cord grass. These species already exist in the system, and we have every reason to believe they will continue to thrive."

As reported in December's Environment DEC, invasive species can have serious consequences for native plants and animals. Invasives come from around the world, with the rate of invasion increasing as international trade expands. They are considered to be second only to habitat loss in threatening our biodiversity.

Student Conservation Association

The SCA provides high schoolers, college students and others with meaningful conservation service internships and volunteer opportunities in our national parks, forests and other public lands. In New York State, SCA members partner with dozens of environmental entities, DEC among them. Through these partnerships, young men and women complete a wide range of projects, such as protecting habitat and watersheds, enhancing trails and educating the public. Those who participated in the Norton Basin cleanup get together each month for conservation service projects or for in-service training.
