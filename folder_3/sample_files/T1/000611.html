What We're Reading

Every other week, the West Linn Tidings and Lake Oswego Review publish book reviews written by West Linn Public Library staff. Click on the titles to find these books at the library.

Mudbound
by Hillary Jordan
This WWII-era novel tells the story of six people and how their lives come together in the Deep South. Laura McAllen’s happy life is turned upside down when her husband, Henry, unexpectedly moves his wife and two daughters to a muddy farm in Mississippi. Henry’s joy at becoming a landowner and working the land is nothing to Laura’s unhappiness at becoming a farm wife. Laura nicknames the farm “Mudbound” for the mud that plagues them and covers everything. Henry’s brother, Jamie, who has just returned from the war mentally broken, provides a small bit of comfort in Laura’s hard new life. The McAllen’s story is interwoven with the stories of Henry’s black sharecroppers Florence and Hap, and their son Ronsel who has also recently returned from the war. These six characters narrate alternate chapters as their stories all head towards an inevitable and horrifying act of racial hatred. This debut novel is a must-read.

-- recommendation from Cheryl Hill, Librarian

"Tales of the New West"
by The Beat Farmers

While San Diego’s legendary Beat Farmers may not be a household name, one listen to their raucous and thoroughly entertaining debut album, "Tales of the New West", makes it clear why they have inspired such a loyal and devoted following over the years. At heart, the Beat Farmers were a bar band, playing tight, roots-based rock, and their 1985 debut crackles with all the energy and fun of the group’s live show. The band effortlessly combines strong original compositions like "Bigger Stones" and "Goldmine", with rollicking covers like Neil Young’s "Powderfinger" and the Velvet Underground’s "There She Goes Again". The band’s quirky sense of humor shines through when gravely-voiced drummer Country Dick Montana takes over vocal duties on tracks like "California Kid" and "Happy Boy". Their later albums can be hard to find; thankfully, this reissued CD includes tracks and demos from the group’s later albums, as well as some live tracks recorded during the band’s peak.

-- recommendation from Greg Williams, Librarian

Society’s Child: My Autobiography
by Janis Ian

A wunderkind at 15, forgotten by 20. For many listeners, that sums up the career of Janis Ian, whose song "Society’s Child" brought her international fame and recognition at an early age, but who found herself struggling mentally, financially and emotionally only a few years later. However, as Ian herself reveals so directly in this memoir, there was much more to her life than this initial blip on the radar screen. While still writing and singing music, she was suffering a nervous breakdown, enduring an abusive marriage, and struggling to define her own sexual identity. She details her financially and emotionally devastating divorce and subsequent tax issues with the same candor and somewhat offhanded voice she employs in relating that she sang the first Egg McMuffin radio spot for McDonald’s – they asked her to make it more "eggy." Nevertheless, fans and casual readers alike will find the book hard to put down.

-- recommendation from Linda V. Malone, Adult Services Senior Librarian

The Spies of Warsaw
by Alan Furst

Alan Furst's latest spy thriller opens in Warsaw, Poland, in late 1937. War looms, and the atmosphere is tense. As winter approaches, "out in the countryside, the first paw prints of wolves were seen near the villages." This novel follows Jean-Francois Mercier de Boutillon, a French aristocrat and veteran of World War I, military attaché in Warsaw, as he studies German preparations for war, runs agents, and performs dangerous field work on his own. His strong commitment to what he’s doing, and frustration with France for not believing solid proof that Hitler plans to invade France by going around the Maginot Line and entering through Belgium feel real. As with Mercier, Furst draws each of his wide range of characters with precision, and describes everything from embassy cocktail receptions to clandestine operations with equal ease. Even though the reader knows the scope of the war that's coming, this spy novel is compelling from the first page.

-- recommendation from Carole Hull, Library Assistant

"Real Animal"
by Alejandro Escovedo

Texas rock icon Alejandro Escovedo boasts a rock resume like no other; he played in a trio of legendary bands (the Nuns, Rank and File, and the True Believers), released some of the most critically acclaimed Americana albums of the past 15 years, and barely survived a battle with Hepatitis-C in 2005. Drawing upon these experiences, Escovedo's latest album, "Real Animal", is a biographical tour-de-force, deftly combining raucous and raw rave-ups with delicate and introspective ballads. On the driving "Chelsea Hotel '78", Escovedo vividly captures the glamour and darkness of punk rock-era New York City, and on songs like "Nuns Song" and "Chip and Tony", Escovedo recalls his musical past and pays tribute to former bandmates. Escovedo’s near-fatal illness informs the lyrics of songs like the bluesy "People (We're Only Gonna Live So Long)" and the haunting "Golden Bear". By mining his past, Escovedo has crafted his strongest solo album yet. "Real Animal" is a career highlight, in every sense of the term.

-- recommendation from Greg Williams, Reference Librarian

Answering 911: Life in the Hot Seat
By Caroline Burau

This memoir is a fascinating look into what it's like to be a 911 dispatcher. Burau’s job is a difficult one, entailing long hours of boredom periodically interrupted by moments of intense stress and fear. As a dispatcher, she is many miles away from the scene of the crisis, yet she is living that crisis moment by moment with the caller. Sometimes, she never finds out the ultimate outcome of a 911 call. That's just part of the job. In addition to the stressful stories of armed robberies, suicides, and accidents are the humorous stories: a person calling to complain about their neighbor's clarinet practice, the woman who wanted the police to babysit her kids, and the person who got lost and called 911 for directions. This is an absorbing sneak peek into a job that most people don’t know much about.

-- recommendation from Cheryl Hill, Reference Librarian
