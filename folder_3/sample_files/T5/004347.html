The course of neurodevelopmental functioning in the infants of IVDA and HIV seropositive parents.

Nozyce M, Diamond G, Belman A, Cabot T, Cohen H, Rubinstein A; International Conference on AIDS.

Int Conf AIDS. 1989 Jun 4-9; 5: 194 (abstract no. M.B.O.41).

Rose F. Kennedy Center, Albert Einstein College of Medicine, Dept. of Pediatrics, Bronx, New York

OBJECTIVE: To study the medical and neurodevelopmental effects of various teratogens on the offspring of high risk and substance abusing parents. METHODS: Pregnant women with both high risk and substance abusing behaviors who attended hospital affiliated clinics were offered enrollment in a multicenter NIH sponsored program. A cohort of 34 infants born to these mothers were followed longitudinally during the first year of life with serial standardized developmental evaluations and medical and neurological assessments. Thirty-two percent of the mothers were seropositive for HIV. A Kent State Adaptation was performed on the Bayley Scales of Infant Development in order to delineate strengths and weaknesses in different areas of functioning. RESULTS: Early results during the first year of life indicate that the general rate of development is slower in the HIV positive group compared with the non-infected group. Prelinguistic language skills were more delayed than other areas of functioning (chi square significant at the less than .05 level). Among the seropositive infants, no regression in developmental skills was noted except during periods of acute illness. Subjects were matched and no significant differences were found in social support, level of maternal education and parity. CONCLUSIONS: Current data indicates that subtle central nervous system (CNS) impairments may be more pervasive earlier than previously documented in HIV seropositive infants and they differ from patterns in older children.
