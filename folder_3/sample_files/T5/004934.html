Baby dies after long wait in casualty
Xavier Bosch
Barcelona
Top
 
The death of a 3 month old Moroccan girl in a public hospital at the Spanish colonial city of Melilla (in north west Africa) has sparked nationwide controversy, because the death seems to have been due to a delay in her being seen in the emergency unit.

Yousra el Founti was taken by her mother to the emergency department of the Hospital Comarcal de Melilla because of shortness of breath. According to her mother, however, it took three hours for the baby to be seen and she died shortly afterwards. Her parents refused a postmortem examination.

The Spanish government is responsible for Melilla, despite the fact that it is situated on the northern coast of Morocco. After questions from Spain's main opposition party the health minister, Celia Villalobos, told the Spanish parliament last week that the baby's death was due not to a medical negligence but to an “organisational error” in prioritisation of emergency cases.

Ms Villalobos admitted that the hospital lacked an analysis unit to enable prioritisation according to gravity of conditions. She based her conclusions on two independent, parallel studies conducted by the hospital itself and a medical inspector of the state health service. The inspector travelled from Madrid to Melilla and took statements from all the health staff who were present at the emergency that night, the baby's parents, and two witnesses.

The results of these studies show that doctors took 1 hour and 22 minutes to visit Yousra. Ms Villalobos said that “the death of the baby was related to the impossibility of assessing correctly the gravity of her condition.”

The family's lawyers said that the recognition of an error by Ms Villalobos showed that the baby's death happened as a consequence of a “negligence or imprudence.”

Ms Villalobos concluded her speech saying that “a new prioritisation unit is already under construction in the hospital and it is planned to finish it by the end of autumn.”

It is now known that emergency doctors at the hospital had been demanding the setting up of a triage unit since April 1998 to determine the gravity of every emergency and the order of attention. Sadly, it seems that it has been only after the death of the baby that the health ministry is giving the go-ahead to such a unit.
